import numpy as np
import pandas as pd

# Forecasting the test set
forecast = sarima_model.get_forecast(steps=len(test))
forecasted_values = forecast.predicted_mean

# Resetting index of the test set to ensure proper alignment
test_reset = test.reset_index(drop=True)

# Recalculating the performance metrics
mae = np.mean(np.abs(forecasted_values.values - test_reset['co2'].values))
mse = np.mean((forecasted_values.values - test_reset['co2'].values)**2)
mape = np.mean(np.abs((forecasted_values.values - test_reset['co2'].values) / test_reset['co2'].values)) * 100

# MASE
naive_forecast = train['co2'].shift(1).dropna()
mase = np.mean(np.abs(forecasted_values.values - test_reset['co2'].values)) / np.mean(np.abs(naive_forecast - train['co2'].iloc[1:]))

4C) 10 pts Summarize the difference between your Recursive forecasts and your h-step ahead forecasts from SARIMA and Auto ARIMA. Explain the methodologies and the reason that you get different results. What are the advantages and disadvantages of the recursive method.

The difference between the recursive forecasts using LSTM and the h-step ahead forecasts from SARIMA and Auto ARIMA can be explained as discussed in the paper A review and comparison of strategies for multi-step ahead forecasting in time series (Ben Taieb et al., 2012). Recursive forecasting with LSTM involves predicting one time step ahead and using that prediction as input for the next step, following a recursive strategy. This method is dynamic and flexible, capable of capturing complex non-linear relationships within the data, but it can lead to error propagation where inaccuracies in early predictions compound in subsequent steps.

This is evident in the higher error metrics observed: MAE of 15.885, MSE of 337.958, MAPE of 3.95, and sMAPE of 4.06%. On the other hand, SARIMA and Auto ARIMA generate h-step ahead forecasts directly, similar to the direct strategy discussed in the paper, where forecasts for future steps are made in a single model run without using previous predictions as inputs. These models assume linearity and seasonality in the data, providing significantly lower error metrics and better performance on the test data.

The paper highlights that while the recursive approach can adapt to changing data patterns and model complex relationships, it is prone to error accumulation. Conversely, the direct approach, akin to SARIMA and Auto ARIMA, avoids this issue by predicting future values in one step, leading to more stable and accurate forecasts. The primary advantage of the recursive method is its flexibility and ability to model non-linear time series, making it useful in capturing intricate data patterns. However, its disadvantages include increased computational complexity, a higher risk of overfitting, and vulnerability to error propagation. SARIMA and Auto ARIMA, using a more traditional direct strategy, are more robust and interpretable for linear and seasonal data patterns, providing more reliable and accurate forecasts without the iterative error accumulation seen in the recursive approach.
