from statsmodels.tsa.stattools import adfuller
def adf_test(series, title=''):
    result = adfuller(series, autolag='AIC')
    print(f'ADF Statistic for {title}: {result[0]}')
    print(f'p-value: {result[1]}')
    for key, value in result[4].items():
        print('Critical Values:')
        print(f'   {key}, {value}')

Part 2 – 40 pts Granger Causality

A. Personal Consumption Expenditures (PCE) represent the total amount of money spent by consumers on goods and services. Average Hourly Earnings (AHE) indicate the average income earned per hour by workers. Intuitively, if consumers have higher earnings (AHE), they are likely to spend more, leading to an increase in PCE. Therefore, it can be argued that changes in AHE might lead to changes in PCE. However, it's also possible that higher consumption (PCE) could drive demand for labor, potentially increasing AHE.

from statsmodels.tsa.stattools import grangercausalitytests, q_stat
def granger_causality_test(data, variables, maxlag=5, alpha=0.05, title=''):
    granger_results = grangercausalitytests(data, maxlag=maxlag, verbose=True)
    res_list = []
    for lag, v in granger_results.items():
        res = {}
        res['Lag'] = lag
        for test, stats in v[0].items():
            res[test] = stats[1]  # p-value
        res_list.append(res)

    pvals = pd.DataFrame(res_list)
    pvals.set_index('Lag', inplace=True)
    pvals_graph = pvals['params_ftest']

    # Plot the p-values
    plt.figure(figsize=(5, 3))
    pvals_graph.plot(title=title)
    alpha_ser = pd.Series([alpha]*len(pvals_graph), index=pvals_graph.index)
    alpha_ser.plot(color='red', linestyle='--')
    plt.ylabel('p-value')
    plt.xlabel('Lag')
    plt.show()

E. 8 pts Summarize your findings. You must understand the underlying null hypothesis of test.

The null hypothesis for the Granger causality test states that the lagged values of one time series do not provide any statistically significant information about another time series.

For all lags tested (1 to 4), the p-values are significantly greater than 0.05.Therefore, we fail to reject the null hypothesis, suggesting that lagged values of AHE_diff2 do not Granger-cause PCE at any of the lags tested. For all lags tested (1 to 4), the p-values are also greater than 0.05.

Similarly, we fail to reject the null hypothesis, suggesting that lagged values of PCE do not Granger-cause AHE_diff2 at any of the lags tested.

The Granger causality tests for both directions do not provide evidence to reject the null hypothesis for any of the lags tested. This indicates that there is no significant Granger causality between AHE_diff2 and PCE in either direction within the lag range considered (1 to 4). Past values of AHE do not contain predictive information about PCE, and past values of PCE do not contain predictive information about AHE (after second differencing).

D. 10 pts What is the advantage of this VAR model? What would ARIMA or prophet models on this dataset look like, if we were asked to forecast AHE?

The VAR model has several advantagesbecause it deals with multiple interdependent time series. It can handle and model multiple time series variables simultaneously. For instance, when forecasting AHE, a VAR model can incorporate the interdependencies between AHE, PCE, and PCEPI. This multivariate approach allows the model to capture the dynamic interactions between these variables, potentially leading to more accurate and holistic forecasts. Additionally, VAR models are flexible in their lag structure, allowing them to capture complex temporal dependencies across multiple variables.

In contrast, if we were to use an ARIMA model to forecast AHE, the approach would be different. ARIMA focuses on a single time series at a time/ univariate. The ARIMA model is effective at capturing patterns within the AHE series itself, such as trends, cycles, and seasonality. It would require us to ensure that the AHE series is stationary, which might involve differencing the data. The model would then be identified and fitted using the historical AHE data alone. While ARIMA can provide accurate forecasts for single time series, it does not account for potential influences from other related variables.

Prophet is designed to handle time series data with strong seasonal effects. It is particularly user-friendly and can automatically detect and model changes in trends, seasonality, and holidays. Prophet's strength lies in its ease of use and its ability to handle missing data and outliers. However, similar to ARIMA, Prophet is primarily a univariate model, although it can incorporate external regressors. This means that in our case,while Prophet would provide a robust forecast for AHE, it might not fully capture the interdependencies with other variables like PCE and PCEPI, which the VAR model naturally does.
